Our Research

Transients


High energy astrophysical transients – astrophysical phenomena that last days, months, years – are pivotal for understanding two areas of fundamental physics - the origin of heavy elements and the nature of the expansion of our cosmos. Most elements heavier than iron are produced in the explosive deaths of stars resulting in said high energy transients (supernovae being a subclass of them). The extreme the energetics of these events also make them observable across vast cosmic distances. Calibration of the intrinsic luminosities of some classes of events has led to the discovery that our Universe is expanding at an accelerating rate – awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2011.

Modeling Stripped-Envelope Supernovae with Physics-Driven Framework

Stripped-envelope supernovae (SESNe) are the explosive deaths of massive stars that have lost their outer hydrogen and sometimes helium layers. These events are key for studying the final stages of stellar evolution, binary interactions, and stellar wind effects of massive stars. However, their origins and explosion mechanisms remain uncertain. [jing_lu] and her collaborators took part in tackling these challenges by studying the synthetic observables of a set of physics-driven explosions of massive stars that potentially lead to SESNe. The study used radiative transfer simulations to model light curves and spectra of SESNe from massive Wolf-Rayet stars with ejecta masses of 4 to 11 solar masses. The results indicate that progenitors with higher ejecta masses produce broader light curves, consistent with some observed SESNe, but their peak luminosities are lower than expected. Methods commonly used to estimate ejecta mass based on radiative-powered light curves were shown to have uncertainties up to 160%, highlighting the need for self-consistent physics-based models. Additionally, the analysis found that helium lines are detectable even in models with very small helium masses, emphasizing the role of radiation field and ejecta composition in shaping spectral features. This study provides a framework for better understanding SESNe and identifies areas for future improvement, including expanding progenitor model grids and enhancing the treatment of nuclear burning and mixing processes in the hydrodynamic simulations. This work provides a step forward for the direct linkage between the SESNe properties and their progenitor stars.

Surviving Companions

The progenitor systems and of and role of binarity in many different classes of supernovae remain an open question. For instance, type Ia supernovae all but require a binary system to occur, but the precise identity of the non-white dwarf star in the system remains an open question. One way of probing supernova progenitor systems is to examine supernova remnants to identify surviving companions of exploded stars. This practice of searching supernova remnants for surviving companions is an ongoing project in the lab. Previous studies of supernova remnants included combining observations of optical telescopes to constrain stellar identities and search for signatures of supernova ejecta interaction (Shields et al. 2023), and high precision astrometry to search for fast moving objects (Shields et al. 2022), peculiar stars predicted by certain promising type Ia progenitor theories.

Gamma - ray transport in stellar explosions

Supernovae and kilonovae produce a lot of radioactive materials in their ejecta.More than half of the Iron Group Elements (IGE) in the Universe are produced in Type Ia supernovae. The γ - rays produced from the decay of these radioactive materials power the supernova for a longer time. About 20 % to 80 % of the supernova ejecta is in radioactive materials in Type Ia supernovae (SNe Ia). And because they have lesser ejecta mass and higher kinetic energy, the possibility of detection of γ - rays are higher in their cases. In TARDIS-γ , we simulate the generation and propagation of γ - rays in the supernova ejecta. We simulate the decay of radioactive isotopes in the supernova ejecta and follow the propagation of the γ - rays as they suffer various interactions with the surroundings materials. These γ - rays are highly energetic (100 keV - 1000 MeV) and mostly suffer Compton scattering. The highly energetic γ - rays (greater than 1022 keV) produces pair of electron and positron. Thus some of the γ -rays escape from the ejecta and some gets deposited. The γ - rays trace the distribution of radioactive isotopes in the ejecta and hence is a wonderful diagnostic of the explosion mechanism and progenitor scenarios. The flexible and modular nature of the code allows to incorporate various additional physics which can be tested against the observations.
